# Fact-Check Guidelines for Business Cases

Reference file for `case-factcheck`. Defines the claim taxonomy, verification standards, and source hierarchy.

## Claim Taxonomy

### 1. Financial Data
Revenue, profit (GAAP vs. non-GAAP), margins, stock prices, market cap, headcount, growth rates, acquisition prices, valuation multiples.

**Common pitfalls:**
- Mixing GAAP and non-GAAP figures without labeling
- Using adjusted vs. unadjusted stock prices
- Confusing fiscal year with calendar year (many companies have non-calendar fiscal years)
- Headcount: end-of-period vs. average vs. FTE vs. including contractors
- Revenue: reported vs. pro forma vs. constant currency
- Acquisition prices: announced price vs. closing price (these often differ due to stock price changes between announcement and close)

### 2. Dates and Timelines
When events occurred, chronological sequence, duration of periods.

**Common pitfalls:**
- Announcement date vs. completion date (acquisitions, product launches, regulatory approvals)
- Confusing quarter-end dates with earnings release dates
- Time zones affecting "when" something happened
- Fiscal quarter numbering (Q1 for some companies starts in February or October)

### 3. Names and Titles
People's names and spellings, job titles at the time of the event, company names (including legal entity vs. brand name), product names.

**Common pitfalls:**
- Name spellings (McKelvey vs. McKelvie; Andreessen vs. Andressen)
- Titles change over time; verify the title at the time of the event described
- Companies rename (Square became Block; Facebook became Meta)
- Subsidiary vs. parent company attribution

### 4. Quotes and Attributions
Direct quotes, paraphrases, who said what, where it was published.

**Common pitfalls:**
- Paraphrases presented as direct quotes
- Quotes taken from secondary sources that altered the original
- Attribution to the wrong person (especially in joint interviews or panel discussions)
- Quotes from earnings calls vs. press releases vs. interviews (different contexts)

### 5. Events and Actions
Corporate announcements, strategic decisions, product launches, layoffs, restructurings.

**Common pitfalls:**
- Announced vs. completed (a company "announced" layoffs does not mean they happened yet)
- Planned vs. actual (projected headcount reductions vs. actual reductions)
- Scope confusion (department layoffs reported as company-wide)

### 6. Market and Industry Data
Market share, rankings, industry statistics, competitor comparisons.

**Common pitfalls:**
- Market definition affects share calculations (global vs. US, total addressable vs. serviceable)
- Rankings from different methodologies may conflict
- Industry statistics from trade associations vs. research firms vs. government sources

## Source Hierarchy

When sources conflict, prefer in this order:

1. **Company SEC filings** (10-K, 10-Q, 8-K, proxy/DEF 14A): most authoritative for company financial data
2. **Earnings releases and investor presentations**: official company data, sometimes more current than SEC filings
3. **Official press releases** (from the company's newsroom or PR Newswire/Business Wire): authoritative for announcements
4. **Court filings and regulatory documents**: authoritative for legal matters
5. **Company IR pages and stock exchanges**: authoritative for stock data
6. **Tier 1 journalism** (WSJ, NYT, Bloomberg, Reuters, FT, The Information): reliable for events, quotes, and context
7. **Industry research firms** (Gartner, IDC, McKinsey, BCG): useful for market data but methodology varies
8. **Tier 2 journalism and trade press**: useful for industry-specific detail but verify key claims upstream

## Verification Standards

### What to Verify
- Every specific number (dollar amounts, percentages, counts, dates)
- Every direct quote
- Every named person's title and spelling
- Every corporate action (acquisition, layoff, product launch)
- Every timeline claim (before/after, during, since)

### What Not to Verify
- Analytical framing and pedagogical choices (these are the instructor's domain)
- Hypothetical scenarios and discussion questions
- General industry context that is common knowledge (e.g., "the tech industry grew rapidly")
- Attributed claims where the case explicitly presents them as one party's assertion rather than established fact (e.g., "Dorsey claimed the company achieved $500K gross profit per employee"). For these, verify only that the person actually made the claim, not whether the claim itself is true.

### Handling Disagreements Between Sources
When two credible sources give different figures:
1. Note both figures and their sources
2. Identify which source is higher in the hierarchy
3. Check whether the discrepancy is due to different definitions (GAAP vs. non-GAAP, fiscal vs. calendar year, etc.)
4. Present the options to the instructor with a recommendation
5. If the discrepancy cannot be resolved, flag it as Disputed

### Precision Standards
- Financial figures: match the precision of the primary source. Do not round $8.975 billion to $9 billion unless the source itself rounds.
- Dates: exact date when available; month and year when exact date is not available; year only as last resort.
- Headcount: note the source's definition (end-of-period, average, FTE) and use it consistently.
- Percentages: one decimal place for margins and growth rates; whole numbers for market share unless the source uses decimals.
