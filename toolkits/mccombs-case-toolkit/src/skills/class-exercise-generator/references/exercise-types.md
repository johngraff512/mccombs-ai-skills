# Class Exercise Type Guide

Design patterns and facilitation guidance for each supported exercise type. Exercises are organized from lightest-weight (polls) to heaviest (gallery walk).

---

## Poll / Vote

**What it is:** A quick question with 2-4 options. Students commit to a position via show-of-hands, clicker, or digital poll.

**Why it works:** Forces every student to take a position, even quiet ones. Creates a data point the instructor can reference throughout the discussion. "Only 30% of you chose Path B — let's hear why."

**When to use:** Opening (surface initial positions), Synthesis (has the discussion changed anyone's mind?), Close (final position).

**Typical duration:** 2-3 minutes (ask + vote + brief reaction).

**Design considerations:**
- Options should be genuinely debatable — avoid obvious answers
- For classes >60, use a digital polling tool rather than show-of-hands (counting is slow and inaccurate)
- Don't discuss results deeply right away — just note the distribution and move on. You'll come back to it.
- A repeat poll at the end of class ("Let's vote again — has anyone shifted?") is a powerful learning moment

**Facilitation notes:**
- "Commit before you look around" — ask students to decide before seeing others
- Note the distribution publicly: "Roughly 40/35/25 split — very even"
- If the vote is lopsided, that's interesting too: "Almost everyone chose A. Let me play devil's advocate for B."

## Short Answer

**What it is:** A focused question requiring a brief verbal response. Instructor cold-calls or takes volunteers.

**Why it works:** Quick pulse check on understanding. Good for surfacing a key insight the instructor wants to build on.

**When to use:** Opening (activate knowledge), Analysis (test understanding of a specific concept), anywhere as a transition.

**Typical duration:** 3-5 minutes (ask + 2-3 responses + brief instructor synthesis).

**Design considerations:**
- The question should have a clear, focused answer — not "What do you think about Nvidia?" but "What is Nvidia's single largest source of revenue?"
- Cold-call is more effective than volunteering for this format — it keeps everyone engaged
- Use to set up a deeper exercise: "Several of you said CUDA. Let's test that — turn to your partner..."

## Think-Pair-Share (TPS)

**What it is:** Three phases: (1) Individual silent reflection on a prompt, (2) Discussion with a partner, (3) Selected pairs share with the class.

**Why it works:** Gives introverts processing time before group discussion. The pair discussion means every student articulates their thinking, not just the vocal ones. The "agree on one answer" constraint forces synthesis.

**When to use:** Analysis (before opening a complex topic to full-class discussion), Synthesis (force a position before debate).

**Typical duration:** 5-8 minutes (Think: 1-2 min, Pair: 2-3 min, Share: 2-3 min).

**Design considerations:**
- The prompt should require a position, not just description: "What is the single most important thing..." not "Discuss CUDA"
- The "agree on one answer" constraint is key — it forces the pair to negotiate and prioritize
- During pair discussion, circulate and eavesdrop. Cold-call pairs who had interesting disagreements.
- Call on 3-4 pairs for the Share phase — don't go around the whole room

**Scaling with class size:**
- Think and Pair phases don't scale — same time regardless of class size
- Share phase: budget 1 min per pair called on; 3-4 pairs is usually enough regardless of class size

## Small Group Breakout

**What it is:** Teams of 3-5 analyze a specific question or dimension of the case, then report findings to the class.

**Why it works:** Divides complex analysis into pieces. Each group becomes the "expert" on their piece. The report-out integrates perspectives and reveals tension.

**When to use:** Analysis (middle of the case discussion, when you need to go deeper).

**Typical duration:** 10-20 minutes.

**Design considerations:**
- Assign different analysis dimensions to different groups — parallel analysis of the same question yields redundant report-outs
- Give each group a specific question AND tell them which case data to use
- Structure the report-out: "State your finding in one sentence, then explain with evidence"
- **Report-out time is the critical bottleneck.** Budget 2 min per reporting group. Select 3-4 groups to report — NOT all groups.
- If class size creates >12 groups, some groups analyze the same dimension. Don't let report-outs exceed 10 min.

**Scaling with class size:**
| Class Size | Groups (at 5/group) | Report-Out Groups | Report-Out Time |
|------------|---------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| 30 | 6 | 3-4 | 6-8 min |
| 60 | 12 | 3-4 | 6-8 min |
| 90 | 18 | 4-5 | 8-10 min |

## Framework Application

**What it is:** Small groups apply a specific analytical framework to the case using a structured worksheet. The framework depends on the discipline — strategy cases use Five Forces, VRIO, BCG, SWOT, or value chain; finance cases use DCF, capital structure analysis, or financial ratio analysis; marketing cases use STP, customer journey mapping, or positioning maps; operations cases use process mapping, bottleneck analysis, or capacity planning; MIS cases use system architecture or build-vs-buy evaluation; accounting cases use audit risk assessment or materiality analysis.

**Why it works:** Hands-on practice with frameworks using real data. The worksheet gives structure; the group discussion builds depth.

**When to use:** Analysis (when a framework is central to the day's learning objective).

**Typical duration:** 12-20 minutes (group work: 8-12 min, report-outs: 4-8 min).

**Design considerations:**
- Provide a partially structured worksheet — labels and categories filled in, content blank
- Option A: All groups apply the same framework, then compare conclusions
- Option B: Different groups apply different frameworks to the same case, then the class compares what each framework reveals
- Ask each group to identify "the single most important force/factor" — this forces prioritization
- Worksheet can be displayed on screen (students use their own paper) or distributed as printed handouts

## Structured Debate

**What it is:** Teams are assigned to argue for a specific position — even if it's not the position they personally favor. The positions depend on the discipline: strategy debates argue for strategic paths; finance debates argue for capital allocation or valuation approaches; marketing debates argue for different targeting or pricing strategies; operations debates argue for process design alternatives. Teams present, rebut, and the class votes.

**Why it works:** Forces students to construct the strongest possible case for a position they may disagree with. Builds argumentation skills and empathy for alternative perspectives.

**When to use:** Synthesis (after sufficient analysis, when students are ready to take positions).

**Typical duration:** 20-35 minutes.

**Typical structure:**
- 2-3 teams (or 2-3 positions with multiple teams per position for large classes)
- 8-12 min team prep
- 3 min presentation per position
- 1 min rebuttal per position
- Class vote + debrief

**Design considerations:**
- Assign positions — don't let teams choose
- Keep presentations strictly timed (3 minutes max)
- The class vote is a conversation starter, not a verdict
- For large classes: multiple teams per position, select 1 team per position to present

**Scaling with class size:**
| Class Size | Teams per Position | Total Prep Groups | Presenting Teams |
|------------|-------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| 30 | 1 | 3 | All 3 |
| 60 | 2 | 6 | 3 (one per position) |
| 90 | 3 | 9 | 3 (one per position) |

## Role Play

**What it is:** Students adopt the perspective of a specific stakeholder and negotiate, present, or make decisions from that viewpoint.

**Why it works:** Develops empathy for different strategic perspectives. Information asymmetry between roles creates authentic tension.

**When to use:** Analysis or Synthesis (when stakeholder dynamics are central to the case).

**Typical duration:** 20-35 minutes.

**Design considerations:**
- Each role card should include background, incentives, constraints, and one piece of information only that role knows
- Include at least one role whose interests genuinely conflict with the protagonist's
- For large classes: parallel role plays in breakout groups, then reconvene for debrief
- The instructor should circulate during role play and note interesting moments

## Decision Memo

**What it is:** Individual (or pair) exercise: write a brief structured memo recommending a course of action to the case protagonist.

**Why it works:** Forces written synthesis. Students can't hide behind vague verbal contributions. Mirrors real business communication.

**When to use:** Opening (bridge from case prep to discussion) or Synthesis (force a commitment after analysis).

**Typical duration:** 12-20 minutes (writing: 8-12 min, share: 4-8 min).

**Design considerations:**
- Strict format: Recommendation (1 sentence) → Key Arguments (3 bullets) → Risks (2 bullets)
- Word/page limit forces clarity (one page or 250 words)
- Written TO someone specific (the CEO, the board)
- Consider having students swap and critique each other's memos

## Gallery Walk

**What it is:** Teams create visual analyses posted around the room. Teams rotate, view, and annotate each other's work.

**Why it works:** Physical movement changes energy. Visual thinking engages different processes than verbal. Rotation forces engagement with other perspectives.

**When to use:** Analysis (when multiple frameworks or dimensions need comparing).

**Typical duration:** 25-40 minutes.

**Design considerations:**
- Different prompts per team — identical posters don't create interesting rotation
- Provide different colored markers for annotations
- During rotation: write one "agree," one "disagree," and one "question" per poster
- Requires physical space and materials — confirm logistics

**Scaling with class size:**
- More students → more groups → more posters → longer rotation
- For classes >60, consider having teams of 6-7 (fewer posters to rotate through)

---

## Exercise Sequencing Principles

1. **Start light, build heavy** — Open with polls/short-answer, build to breakouts, peak with flagship
2. **Alternate energy levels** — Individual (reflective) → group (active) → pair (moderate) → group (active)
3. **Each exercise should reference the previous one** — "In our breakout, several groups noted X. Now let's test whether that holds up in a debate."
4. **Leave time for instructor-led discussion** — exercises are ~60% of class time; instructor-led discussion fills the rest
5. **The closing exercise should create resolution** — a final vote, a "one takeaway" TPS, or a provocative question to leave with
