# Teaching Note Writing Guidelines

This document provides the pedagogical framework and structural guidelines for writing effective teaching notes. A teaching note is an instructor-only document — it should give any qualified instructor enough guidance to teach the case well, even if they didn't write it.

## Purpose of a Teaching Note

A teaching note answers three questions:
1. **Why teach this case?** What will students learn, and where does it fit in a course?
2. **How should I prepare?** What analysis should the instructor do before class?
3. **How should I run the session?** What questions to ask, in what order, and what to capture?

The best teaching notes read like advice from an experienced colleague who has already taught the case several times.

## Section-by-Section Guidelines

### Synopsis (2-3 paragraphs)

A neutral summary of the case. Should be:
- Descriptive, not analytical — describe what happens, not what it means
- Free of judgment about which option is "right"
- Concise enough that an instructor can use it to quickly brief themselves before class

Do NOT include spoilers if there is an epilogue. The synopsis covers only what students see.

### Learning Objectives

Restate the objectives from the case, then add a brief line of pedagogical context for each: what does it look like when a student *demonstrates* this objective? This helps instructors recognize when the discussion has achieved its goal.

**Examples by discipline:**
- Strategy — Objective: "Evaluate how Nvidia builds and sustains competitive advantage by applying Porter's Five Forces and resource-based view frameworks." Context: "Students demonstrate this when they can distinguish between replicable and sticky sources of advantage."
- Finance — Objective: "Analyze the optimal capital structure for the acquisition using trade-off theory and DCF valuation." Context: "Students demonstrate this when they can quantify the impact of leverage on firm value and articulate the risks."
- Marketing — Objective: "Evaluate the company's repositioning strategy using STP analysis and brand equity frameworks." Context: "Students demonstrate this when they can identify which customer segments are most attractive and why."
- Operations — Objective: "Assess the supply chain redesign using process mapping and bottleneck analysis." Context: "Students demonstrate this when they can identify the binding constraint and evaluate alternative process configurations."

### Suggested Positioning

Help instructors understand where this case fits in a course:

- **Suggested courses**: Which courses or modules is this case well-suited to? (e.g., "MBA Strategic Management," "Financial Analysis & Valuation," "Digital Marketing Strategy," "Operations & Supply Chain Management," "Information Systems & Technology")
- **Where in curriculum**: Early in the course for foundational concepts; mid-course when students have frameworks but need synthesis practice; late course for integration cases
- **Prerequisite topics**: What frameworks or concepts should students already know before tackling this case? Don't assign a case requiring Five Forces if students haven't encountered it yet
- **Paired readings**: Academic articles or textbook chapters that pair well with the case. Include author, title, and publication year

### Pre-Class Assignment Questions

These are questions distributed to students BEFORE class to guide their preparation. They are NOT the same as in-class discussion questions — they are simpler, more directive, and designed to ensure students come prepared.

Good pre-class questions:
- Can be answered with 30-60 minutes of careful case reading
- Direct attention toward the most important aspects of the case
- Often ask students to take a position they'll need to defend in class
- Should not be so detailed that they constrain the in-class discussion

Typically 2-4 questions. Example:
- "What is Nvidia's primary source of competitive advantage, and how durable is it?"
- "Which of the three strategic paths would you recommend to Jensen Huang, and why? Be prepared to defend your position."

### Teaching Plan

A session timeline in table format. Each row represents a phase of the class discussion:

| Time | Phase | Activity | Instructor Goal |
|------|-------|----------|-----------------|
| 0–10 min | Opening | Cold call 2-3 students on pre-class Q1. Map divergent views. | Surface the core tension early; avoid premature consensus |
| 10–30 min | Analysis | Small group: apply Five Forces to Nvidia | Students discover the sources and limits of the moat themselves |

**Guidance on timing:**
- Opening: 10-15% of session (set up, cold calls, surface the problem)
- Analysis: 40-50% of session (the core work — frameworks, evidence, debate)
- Synthesis: 20-30% of session (integrate insights, move toward a decision)
- Wrap-up: 10-15% of session (debrief, key takeaways, connections to frameworks)

For a 75-minute session, a typical structure:
- 0–10: Opening / cold call
- 10–30: Competitive analysis (frameworks)
- 30–50: The competitive threats (evidence, debate)
- 50–65: Strategic options (evaluation)
- 65–75: Wrap-up / synthesis

For a 90-minute session, add 15 minutes — typically by expanding the analysis phase or adding a small group exercise.

### Discussion Questions + Analysis

This is the heart of the teaching note. For each discussion question, provide:

**Question**: The exact question to ask (or a strong version of it). Phrase it so it's genuinely open-ended — students should be able to defend multiple positions.

**Timing**: When in the session to ask this question.

**Purpose**: Why this question at this point in the discussion? What insight should emerge?

**Key teaching points**: What the instructor should draw out — not what students will say, but what the class should *arrive at* by the end of this discussion segment. These are the concepts and frameworks to make explicit.

**Anticipated responses**: What students will actually say in response to this question. Often different from the key teaching points — students lead with surface observations and the instructor has to probe deeper. Note common misconceptions to address.

**Board capture**: A note on what to write on the board after this discussion segment closes. The board plan is built incrementally through the teaching plan.

**Discussion question order matters.** Structure the sequence to build insight progressively:
1. **Open** with a concrete, grounded question — what is happening, who is involved
2. **Analyze** — apply frameworks to understand why (competitive structure, strategic position)
3. **Evaluate** — assess options, trade-offs, stakeholder perspectives
4. **Synthesize** — draw conclusions, make recommendations, connect to broader principles

Avoid jumping to "what should they do?" before students have a shared analytical foundation. Premature evaluation without shared analysis leads to opinion-swapping, not learning.

### Board Plan

The board plan is the visual record of the class discussion. It should tell the story of what the class discovered together.

Describe:
- The overall layout (number of columns, what each column represents)
- What goes in each column, and when it gets added
- Any visual connections between columns (arrows, groupings)

A good board plan at the end of class is a photograph students want to take. It captures the key tensions, the analytical framework applied, and the strategic options considered.

Common patterns (adapt to the discipline):
- **Three-column**: Situation | Analysis | Options/Recommendations (Strategy: Sources of Advantage | Threats | Strategic Options. Finance: Valuation Drivers | Risk Factors | Capital Allocation Options. Marketing: Customer Segments | Value Propositions | Go-to-Market Approaches.)
- **Two-column**: Arguments for Option A | Arguments for Option B
- **Matrix**: 2x2 with trade-offs on each axis
- **Timeline**: How the situation evolved and what forces drove it

The board plan in the teaching note should be a description + a list of items for each section — not a literal diagram.

### Epilogue (Optional)

The epilogue reveals what actually happened after the case's decision point. Include it when:
- The outcome is known and instructive (confirms, surprises, or complicates the analysis)
- The instructor wants to close the loop for students
- The outcome itself is a teaching moment

Keep it brief (1-2 paragraphs). The instructor controls when to reveal it — some save it for after students have defended their recommendations, to show whether they were right and why.

If the outcome is not yet known (the case is about very recent events), note this: "As of [date], no public announcement has been made. Instructors may wish to update this section if developments occur."

## Quality Standards

A strong teaching note:
- Enables any qualified instructor to teach the case effectively, even without prior preparation
- Separates what students will *say* from what they should *learn* (anticipated responses vs. teaching points)
- Gives honest timing estimates — 20 minutes for a complex analysis discussion, not 5
- Acknowledges that students will have diverse views and coaches the instructor on how to use that productively
- Is written in an instructor-to-instructor voice: collegial, direct, practical

A weak teaching note:
- Just lists topics without guidance on how to draw them out
- Treats anticipated student responses as uniformly correct or smart
- Gives a discussion plan so rigid it doesn't leave room for where the actual conversation goes
- Reveals a "right answer" prematurely instead of guiding discovery
