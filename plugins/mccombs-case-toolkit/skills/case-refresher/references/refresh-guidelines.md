# Case Refresh Guidelines

This document provides guidance on assessing whether a case needs refreshing and how to prioritize updates.

---

## When Does a Case Need Refreshing?

Not every case needs updating. Cases are pedagogical tools, not news articles. A case should be refreshed when:

1. **Key financial data is more than 2 years old** and the numbers have changed materially (not just incrementally)
2. **The competitive landscape has shifted** — major new entrants, exits, acquisitions, or market share changes
3. **The protagonist has made the decision** the case asks students to analyze — the case is no longer "live"
4. **Regulatory or policy changes** have altered the decision environment
5. **A major event** has occurred that students will know about and that changes the analysis (e.g., a company went public, faced a crisis, made a major acquisition)

A case does NOT need refreshing just because time has passed. If the core dilemma, data, and competitive dynamics are still valid, the case is still current.

---

## Dilemma Status Assessment

The most important question in a case refresh: **Is the central dilemma still live?**

### Status: Live
The protagonist has not yet made the decision, or the decision is still being implemented. The case's options are all still on the table.

**Teaching implication:** The case works as originally designed. Updates add context but don't change the discussion structure.

### Status: Resolved
The protagonist has clearly chosen one path. The outcome may or may not be known.

**Teaching implication:** The case becomes partly historical. The instructor can:
- Teach the case as-is, then reveal the actual decision as an epilogue
- Ask students: "The CEO chose Path A. Was that the right call? What evidence supports or challenges that choice?"
- Use the actual outcome to test the quality of students' analysis

### Status: Evolved
The dilemma has changed shape. The original options may no longer be the right framing — new options have emerged, or the decision environment has shifted so much that the original analysis needs updating.

**Teaching implication:** This is where a full case refresh (Mode A) is most valuable. The instructor may need updated options, revised exhibits, and new competitive context. A refresh memo (Mode B) should flag this clearly and suggest how to adapt the discussion.

## Materiality Thresholds

Not every change is worth including. Use these thresholds to assess materiality:

### Financial Data
| Metric | Material if changed by... |
|--------|--------------------------|
| Revenue | >20% from case figures |
| Market share | >5 percentage points |
| Gross margin | >5 percentage points |
| Stock price | >50% (either direction) |
| Employee count | >30% |
| Market cap | >50% |

### Competitive / Market Landscape
- A new top-5 competitor has entered the market (Strategy, Marketing)
- A major competitor has exited, been acquired, or pivoted (Strategy, Marketing)
- A technology shift has changed competitive or market dynamics (Strategy, Operations, MIS)
- A major partnership or alliance has formed (all disciplines)
- A credit rating change or capital markets shift (Finance)
- A significant customer preference or demographic shift (Marketing)
- A supply chain disruption or new enabling technology (Operations)
- A platform emergence or security threat landscape change (MIS)
- A new accounting standard or enforcement action (Accounting)

### Protagonist Actions
- A significant acquisition (>10% of revenue) (Strategy, Finance)
- A major product launch that changes market positioning (Strategy, Marketing)
- A strategic pivot or restructuring (Strategy, Operations)
- Leadership change (new CEO, CFO, CMO, CTO, or founder departure) (all disciplines)
- Capital structure change, dividend policy change, or major financing event (Finance)
- Brand repositioning or go-to-market shift (Marketing)
- Process redesign, supplier change, or capacity expansion (Operations)
- System migration, data governance change, or technology adoption (MIS)
- Accounting method change, restatement, or internal control enhancement (Accounting)

### Regulatory/Policy
- New regulation directly affecting the company or industry
- Antitrust action or investigation
- Trade policy changes (tariffs, export controls)
- Court rulings affecting the business model

## Impact Assessment Framework

For each update, assign an impact level:

### High Impact
The update changes how students should analyze the case. It makes one option clearly better/worse, introduces a new constraint, or resolves the central dilemma.

**Examples:**
- The company chose Path B and the results are now visible
- A new regulation eliminated one of the strategic options
- A competitor's failure validated one argument from the case

### Medium Impact
The update adds important context but doesn't fundamentally change the analysis. Students should know about it, but it doesn't invalidate any position.

**Examples:**
- Revenue grew 30% (confirms the trend in the case but doesn't change the dilemma)
- A new competitor entered but hasn't yet disrupted the market
- The CEO made a public statement clarifying their strategic direction

### Low Impact
Interesting but not essential. Include only if it adds color or if the instructor specifically asked about this area.

**Examples:**
- Minor product updates or feature launches
- Small partnerships or investments
- Industry awards or rankings
- Personnel changes below C-suite

## Source Quality for Refresh Data

Prioritize sources in this order:

1. **SEC filings** (10-K, 10-Q, proxy statements) — most reliable for financial data
2. **Earnings call transcripts** — direct quotes from management on strategy
3. **Company press releases** — official announcements, but note they're self-serving
4. **Major business press** (WSJ, Bloomberg, FT, Reuters) — reliable for context and competitive analysis
5. **Industry analyst reports** (Gartner, IDC, McKinsey) — useful for market data and trends
6. **Trade publications** — good for industry-specific developments
7. **Company blog/website** — useful for product announcements, but treat as primary source material, not objective analysis

Always include the date of the source. A March 2024 article is less useful than a January 2026 earnings release.

## Refresh Memo Structure

A refresh memo should be organized to help the instructor quickly assess what has changed and decide whether to update their teaching approach.

**Recommended structure:**

1. **Executive Summary** (1 paragraph) — What has changed since the case was written? Is the dilemma still live?
2. **Dilemma Status** — Live, Resolved, or Evolved, with explanation
3. **Key Updates** — Organized by category (Financial, Competitive, Strategic, Regulatory), each with impact assessment
4. **Updated Exhibits** — Revised data tables with current figures alongside original case figures for comparison
5. **Teaching Implications** — Practical guidance on how to adjust the case discussion
6. **Sources** — Full citations for all updates

The memo should be concise — typically 3-5 pages. The instructor should be able to read it in 10-15 minutes and know exactly what has changed and what to do about it.
