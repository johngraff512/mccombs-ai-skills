# Case Idea Quality Guide

This document provides criteria for evaluating whether a company/situation makes a strong teaching case idea. Use it during Phase 2 (research) and Phase 3 (structuring) to filter and refine ideas.

---

## The Dilemma Test

The single most important quality of a case idea is the **dilemma**. A good teaching case is NOT a company profile — it's a decision under uncertainty.

**Strong dilemma indicators:**
- The protagonist faces a specific decision with at least 2 defensible paths
- Reasonable, well-informed people could disagree on the right course of action
- The decision involves real trade-offs (not just "do the obvious thing")
- There is time pressure or a forcing function (competitive threat, board meeting, contract deadline)
- The outcome is uncertain — the "right answer" depends on assumptions about the future

**Weak dilemma indicators (avoid these):**
- "Company X is doing interesting things" — no decision point
- "Company X failed because of bad management" — hindsight makes the answer obvious
- The situation has a clearly correct answer that any competent strategist would choose
- The dilemma is purely operational (how to execute) rather than strategic (what to do)

---

## Protagonist Quality

A case needs a protagonist — a named human being making a decision. Abstract "the company decided" is not a case; "CEO Maria Chen must decide whether to..." is.

**Strong protagonist indicators:**
- Named individual with a specific title and role
- Their decision authority is clear
- Their background and incentives create interesting tension (e.g., a founder choosing between growth and control)
- They face constraints that make the decision harder (board pressure, competitive timing, resource limits)

**Protagonist sources:**
- CEO / founder (most common)
- Division or product head (good for focused decisions)
- Board member or investor (good for governance cases)
- Mid-level manager (good for organizational behavior cases)

## Framework Fit

A case idea is more teachable when it naturally maps to specific analytical frameworks. This doesn't mean the case should be a framework illustration exercise — it means the frameworks should help students think about the dilemma more clearly.

**Strong framework connections:**
- The situation raises questions that a framework is designed to answer
- Multiple frameworks apply, and they may suggest different conclusions (this is pedagogically rich)
- The framework application is non-trivial — students have to work through it, not just label boxes

**Common frameworks by discipline** (see `../shared/discipline-guide.md` for comprehensive lists):

*Strategy:* Porter's Five Forces, VRIO / Resource-Based View, Value Chain Analysis, Platform Economics / Network Effects, Game Theory, BCG Matrix, Blue Ocean Strategy, Dynamic Capabilities, Transaction Cost Economics, Stakeholder Analysis

*Finance:* DCF / Net Present Value, WACC, Capital Structure Analysis, Financial Ratio Analysis, Comparable Company Analysis, CAPM / Risk-Return, Dividend Policy, Working Capital Management

*Marketing:* STP (Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning), Marketing Mix (4Ps/7Ps), Customer Lifetime Value, Brand Equity, Customer Journey Mapping, Pricing Strategy, Diffusion of Innovation

*Operations:* Process Mapping, Lean / Six Sigma, Capacity Planning, Supply Chain Design, Quality Management, Inventory Models, Make vs. Buy

*MIS:* System Architecture, Data Governance, Cybersecurity Risk Assessment, Technology Adoption Models, Build vs. Buy vs. SaaS, Digital Transformation

*Accounting:* Revenue Recognition (ASC 606), Audit Risk Model, Materiality Assessment, Fair Value Measurement, Internal Controls (COSO), Ethical Decision-Making

## Data Availability Assessment

A case idea is only useful if it can be developed into a full case with real data. Assess each of these dimensions:

| Data type | Where to find it | Quality threshold |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Financial data** | 10-K/10-Q filings, annual reports, S-1 (if recent IPO) | At least 2-3 years of key metrics (revenue, margins, growth rates) |
| **Industry context** | Industry reports (IBISWorld, Statista, McKinsey), trade publications | Enough to describe competitive landscape and market dynamics |
| **Company strategy** | Earnings calls, CEO interviews, conference presentations, company blog | Direct quotes from protagonist or close associates |
| **Competitive landscape** | Analyst reports, press coverage, competitor filings | Can identify 3-5 key competitors and their positioning |
| **The specific dilemma** | News articles, analyst commentary, industry blogs | External observers have discussed the decision/trade-off |

**Data availability ratings:**
- **High**: Public company with SEC filings + extensive press coverage + analyst reports. Case could be written primarily from public sources.
- **Medium**: Sufficient public data for the core narrative, but some gaps (e.g., limited financial detail, or the dilemma is inferred rather than explicitly discussed). May need to supplement with industry data.
- **Low**: Limited public information. The situation is interesting but would require primary research (interviews, field work) to develop fully. Flag this — it may still be worth pursuing but the instructor should know the effort required.

## Timeliness Assessment

Consider how the passage of time affects the case idea:

- **Current (happening now)**: High student engagement, but risk that the situation resolves before the case is written or taught. Best for cases where the dilemma is structural (won't be resolved by a single announcement).
- **Recent (last 1-2 years)**: Sweet spot — enough data to write a complete case, outcome may or may not be known, still feels relevant to students.
- **Historical (3+ years ago)**: Outcome is known, which enables epilogue discussion. Lower engagement risk ("that's old news") but some historical cases are timeless. Best when the underlying strategic tension is still relevant today.

---

## Student Engagement Factors

A case idea that checks every analytical box but bores students is a missed opportunity. Consider:

- **Brand recognition**: Students are more engaged with companies they know and use (Apple, Netflix, Tesla) but less-known companies can work if the dilemma is compelling
- **Industry relevance**: AI, fintech, healthcare tech, sustainability — industries students care about and may work in
- **Stakes**: Higher-stakes decisions (company survival, billion-dollar bets, industry transformation) generate more engagement than incremental choices
- **Counterintuitive elements**: Situations where the "obvious" answer turns out to be wrong, or where success creates new problems
- **Debate potential**: Cases that naturally split a classroom — where students can argue for different positions with conviction

---

## Idea Comparison Criteria

When choosing among multiple potential ideas, rank them on:

1. **Dilemma quality** (most important): Is this a genuine, debatable strategic decision?
2. **Data availability**: Can a full case actually be written from available sources?
3. **Framework fit**: Does it teach what the instructor needs it to teach?
4. **Student engagement**: Will students care about this company and decision?
5. **Timeliness**: Is the timing right for developing and teaching this case?
