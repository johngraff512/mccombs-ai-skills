# Business Case Writing Guidelines

This document provides the pedagogical framework and structural guidelines for writing effective business cases. Read this before writing any case content.

## What Makes a Good Business Case

A business case is not a report, an article, or a textbook chapter. It is a story about a real person facing a real decision, written to provoke discussion and analysis. The best cases share these qualities:

1. **A clear protagonist** who has the authority to make the decision. Name them. Give them a title. The reader should be able to picture sitting in their office.

2. **A genuine dilemma** with at least two defensible courses of action. If there's an obvious right answer, it's not a good case. The tensions should be real: short-term vs. long-term, growth vs. profitability, stakeholder vs. stakeholder, values vs. pragmatism.

3. **Incomplete information** that forces judgment calls. Real business decisions happen with imperfect data. The case should provide enough information to analyze but not enough to be certain.

4. **Current relevance** that makes students care. Cases based on recent events (within 6-12 months) feel urgent. Students engage more when they know the decision hasn't been fully resolved or when the outcome is still unfolding.

5. **Appropriate complexity** for the audience. An undergraduate case might focus on a single framework application. An MBA case should require integrating multiple perspectives. An EMBA case can assume deep professional experience and focus on executive-level judgment.

## Company-Specific Case Structure

### Learning Objectives Block
Before the case narrative begins, include a "Learning Objectives" section listing the 2-5 objectives the instructor provided. These must use Bloom's taxonomy action verbs (evaluate, analyze, assess, apply, create, etc.) and specify both the skill and the framework or context. This block serves two purposes: it signals to students what they should focus on while reading, and it helps the instructor confirm alignment between the case content and their teaching goals.

### Opening Hook (1-2 paragraphs)
Drop the reader into the moment of decision. Use present tense or recent past. Name the protagonist, their role, and the immediate pressure they face.

**Example approach:** "In March 2025, Satya Nadella sat across from Microsoft's board of directors with a proposal that would reshape the company's AI strategy..."

Avoid generic openings. Don't start with "Company X is a leading provider of..." Start with tension.

### Company and Industry Background (1-2 pages)
Provide the context a reader needs to understand the dilemma. Include:

- Brief company history (founding, key milestones, current market position)
- Industry dynamics (competitive landscape, trends, regulatory environment)
- Key players and stakeholders (executives, competitors, customers, regulators)
- Recent performance (revenue, growth trajectory, market share)
- Historical context for the current situation

Write this as narrative, not as a data dump. Weave the background into a story that builds toward the dilemma.

### The Dilemma (1-2 pages)
This is the heart of the case. Present:

- **What happened**: The event, decision point, or emerging challenge
- **The options**: At least 2 viable paths forward, each with real trade-offs
- **Stakeholder perspectives**: Different people want different things, and their reasons are legitimate
- **Constraints**: Time pressure, resource limitations, competitive threats, organizational politics
- **Tensions**: The fundamental trade-offs that make the decision difficult

Do not editorialize. Present the facts and let the reader form their own analysis.

### Supporting Evidence (integrated throughout + exhibits)
Throughout the case, ground claims in evidence:

- Direct quotes from executives, analysts, or stakeholders
- Financial data (revenue, margins, market cap, growth rates)
- Market data (share, customer metrics, competitive positioning)
- Operational data (capacity, efficiency, workforce)

For quantitative data that would interrupt the narrative, place it in exhibits at the end.

### Exhibits (1-2 pages)
Common exhibit types vary by discipline:
- Financial summary tables (3-5 years of key metrics) — all disciplines
- Competitive comparison matrices — Strategy, Marketing
- Market share or industry structure charts — Strategy, Marketing
- Timeline of key events — all disciplines
- Organizational structure — Management, Strategy
- Valuation models, capital structure comparisons, ratio analysis — Finance
- Customer segmentation data, pricing grids, survey results — Marketing
- Process flow diagrams, capacity/utilization data, quality metrics — Operations
- System architecture diagrams, technology comparison matrices — MIS
- Financial statement extracts with line-item detail, audit findings — Accounting

Label exhibits clearly (Exhibit 1, Exhibit 2, etc.) and reference them in the text.

### Closing (1-2 paragraphs)
Restate the decision to be made. End with a question or a moment of decision.

**Example approach:** "As the board waited for her recommendation, Nadella weighed the $10 billion commitment against the uncertain regulatory landscape. The decision would define Microsoft's competitive position for the next decade."

Do NOT resolve the dilemma. Do NOT hint at what the protagonist should do. The case ends with the question open.

## Topic-Specific Case Structure

### Opening (1-2 paragraphs)
Frame the topic as a current, consequential business challenge. Why should a business leader care about this right now?

### Concept Overview (1 page)
Define the topic, key terms, and relevant frameworks. This section teaches the background knowledge students need to analyze the examples. Keep it concise and practical.

### Company Examples (1-2 pages per company)
Present 2-3 companies that took different approaches to the topic. For each:
- What was their situation?
- What approach did they choose?
- What were the results?
- What trade-offs did they accept?

Choose companies that illustrate genuine strategic differences, not just variations on the same approach.

### Comparative Analysis (1 page)
Synthesize across the examples. What patterns emerge? Where do the approaches conflict? What contextual factors explain different outcomes?

Highlight that there is no universal "best practice" — the right approach depends on context, strategy, and values.

### Closing Dilemma (1-2 paragraphs)
Pose the decision to the reader: "Given these examples, what would you recommend for a company in [situation]?" Make it concrete enough to analyze but open enough to debate.

## Writing Style Guide

### Voice and Tone
- Write in third person, past tense (or present tense for very recent events)
- Use active voice ("The CEO decided" not "A decision was made")
- Be specific, not vague ("Revenue fell 12% to $3.2 billion" not "Revenue declined significantly")
- Write with the authority of good business journalism

### What to Avoid
- Jargon without explanation (especially for undergraduate cases)
- Editorializing or signaling which option is "right"
- Excessive length — if a section doesn't serve the dilemma, cut it
- Hypothetical scenarios presented as fact
- Unattributed claims or statistics

### Length Targets
| Course Level | Text | Exhibits | Total |
|-------------|------|----------|-------|
| Undergraduate | 2-3 pages | 1 page | 3-4 pages |
| MBA | 3-5 pages | 1-2 pages | 4-7 pages |
| Executive MBA | 3-5 pages | 1-2 pages | 4-7 pages |

### Bloom's Taxonomy Alignment
The case should support the instructor's learning objectives at the appropriate cognitive level:

- **Remember/Understand**: Case provides facts and concepts for students to identify and explain
- **Apply**: Case scenario requires students to use a specific framework or tool
- **Analyze**: Case data requires breaking down into components, identifying relationships
- **Evaluate**: Case demands weighing options, making judgments with incomplete information
- **Create**: Case asks students to develop new strategies, plans, or solutions

Most MBA and EMBA cases should target Analyze, Evaluate, or Create levels.

## Sources and Attribution

The Sources section is the **primary citation mechanism** for the case. Every specific data point — industry figures, company financials, market share statistics, executive quotes, product announcements — must be traceable to a numbered entry in the Sources list. Do not include data in the case that does not have a corresponding source.

**Format for each source entry:**
- Author (if known). "Article Title." Publication, Date. URL.
- For company data: Company Name. Annual Report, Fiscal Year 20XX. [Investor Relations URL]
- For analyst estimates: Firm Name. "Report Title." Date.

**Inline attribution:** Within the case body, attribute specific claims to their source naturally in the prose. Examples:
- "Revenue grew to $130.5 billion in fiscal 2025, per Nvidia's annual report..."
- "Custom ASIC shipments were projected to grow 44.6% in 2026, compared to 16.1% for GPUs, according to industry analysts..."
- "Huang estimated the Chinese market could be worth up to $50 billion annually..."

The inline attribution tells the reader where a claim comes from; the Sources section provides the full citation for verification. Every source referenced in the body or exhibits must appear in the Sources list, and every Sources entry should be referenced somewhere in the case.

## Footnotes

Footnotes are **not** a general citation tool — the Sources section handles that. Use footnotes only when a specific claim requires clarifying context that would break the narrative flow if written inline. The bar should be high: most cases need zero to two footnotes at most.

**Use a footnote when:**
- A quote or statistic was made in a broader context that changes its meaning if read in isolation
- A term used in the case was used differently by the original speaker (e.g., an executive used a phrase as macro framing, not as a product specification)
- A claim reflects one interpretation of ambiguous or conflicting data worth flagging

**Do NOT use a footnote when:**
- You simply want to cite the source for a statistic — handle that with inline attribution and the Sources section
- The clarification can be worked naturally into the sentence

**Accuracy standard:** Every specific factual claim — especially product specifications, financial figures, direct quotes, and event details — should be verifiable. If research reveals a fact is more nuanced than it first appeared, use a footnote to clarify the precise context rather than overstating or omitting the nuance.

**Footnote format in the JSON:**
```json
"footnotes": {
  "1": "Lisa Su, AMD CES 2026 Keynote Address, January 6, 2026. Su described the AI industry as entering a 'YottaScale' era — yottaflop-level compute capacity projected over the next five years — as a macro framing for AMD's roadmap, not as a specification of the MI455 or Helios products at launch."
}
```

And in the paragraph `runs` array, insert `{ "type": "footnote_ref", "id": 1 }` immediately after the specific claim that needs clarification.
